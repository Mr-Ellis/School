B1.1.2 – Computational Thinking

📌 Learning Objective

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Define and explain the four core concepts of computational thinking: Abstraction, Algorithmic Design, Decomposition, and Pattern Recognition
  • Provide real-world examples of how these concepts are applied in various situations
  • Analyse a problem and identify how computational thinking concepts can be used to solve it
  • Differentiate between the four core concepts and explain their interrelationships

Describe – "Give a detailed account of the features or characteristics of each concept. Name, define, and give an example."

🔑 Key Terms

TermDefinition
Computational ThinkingA method of problem-solving that helps computer scientists prepare problems for digital solutions.
DecompositionBreaking down a complex problem into smaller, more manageable parts.
Pattern RecognitionIdentifying similarities, trends, or recurring patterns in data or processes.
AbstractionFiltering out unnecessary detail to focus on the essential information.
Algorithmic DesignCreating a step-by-step set of instructions to solve a problem.

📊 The Four Concepts of Computational Thinking

ConceptDescriptionExample
Decomposition Breaking a complex problem into smaller, manageable sub-problems that can each be understood, solved, and tested separately. Building a website: design layout, write HTML, style with CSS, add interactivity, test and deploy.
Pattern Recognition Identifying similarities, regularities, or recurring structures within a single problem or across different problems. Spam detection: patterns in email headers, sender reputation, and keyword frequency recur across spam messages.
Abstraction Representing a problem or system by retaining only the details that are relevant to the task and deliberately omitting those that are not. Student record: abstracts a real person to ID, name, grades, attendance – ignoring hair colour, height, and shoe size.
Algorithmic Design Constructing a finite, ordered sequence of unambiguous instructions that solves a problem or achieves a goal. Binary search: compare to target midpoint, eliminate half the list, repeat – works correctly on any sorted list.

🔄 How They Work Together

ConceptRelationship
DecompositionThe starting point – break the problem down.
Pattern RecognitionFind similarities – apply known solutions.
AbstractionSimplify – focus on what matters.
Algorithmic DesignThe final step – create the solution.

These concepts are rarely applied in isolation or in strict sequence. In practice, they are applied repeatedly and in combination, refining a solution as understanding of the problem deepens.

💡 Key Takeaway

"Computational thinking is a powerful problem-solving approach that can be applied to any field—not just computer science."

The four concepts of computational thinking – decomposition, pattern recognition, abstraction, and algorithmic design – are applied together, and often iteratively, to analyse problems and design solutions. Though rooted in computer science, these concepts underpin systematic thinking across every discipline.
